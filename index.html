I am a Master’s student in Computer Science at the University of Maryland, Baltimore County (UMBC), with a bachelor’s degree in Computer Science. Currently, I am working as a research assistant at the Center for Accelerated Real Time Analytics under the supervision of Milton Halem. I am interested in machine learning, deep learning, physics-informed neural networks, transformers, and foundation models for scientific applications. My research explores AI-driven weather and climate forecasting, using deep learning methods.

Research Interests

I have developed an AI-based OSSE (Observing System Simulation Experiments) framework for weather models to evaluate how new or prospective satellite observing systems and assimilation strategies influence forecast skill and long-range stability. In this framework, synthetic observations are generated from “truth” datasets, selectively assimilated into AI forecast models during inference, and their downstream effects on error growth and regional skill are quantified. An abstract based on this framework has been accepted to the American Meteorological Society (AMS) for presentation. For reproducibility, I maintain lightweight research codebases: Aurora OSSE (code) and FourCastNet-v2 OSSE (code).

Master’s Thesis (in progress): Fine-tuning Aurora (1.3 billion parameters) on CERRA reanalysis at 5.5 km over Europe to increase spatial resolution, improve regional downscaling skill, and advance high-resolution regional AI-based forecasting. Aurora is Microsoft’s foundation model for the Earth system: pretrained on over one million hours of diverse geophysical data and designed to be adapted to multiple tasks (high-resolution weather, air quality, ocean waves) with modest fine-tuning cost; it delivers competitive accuracy at far lower computational expense than traditional NWP and supports specialized high-resolution forecasting workflows. My work adapts Aurora to CERRA’s 5.5 km grid, benchmarking regional skill, transferability across variables, and runtime/throughput gains relevant for operational downscaling.
