I am a Master’s student in Computer Science at the University of Maryland, Baltimore County (UMBC), with a bachelor’s degree in Computer Science (AI&ML). Currently, I am working as a research assistant at the Center for Accelerated Real Time Analytics (CARTA) under the supervision of Dr. Milton Halem. I am interested in machine learning, deep learning, physics-informed neural networks, transformers, and foundation models for scientific applications. My research explores AI-driven weather and climate forecasting, using deep learning methods for long-range prediction.

Research Interests

My research is focused on advancing AI-driven weather and climate forecasting through the development and evaluation of physics-informed neural operators and transformer-based architectures. I work with state-of-the-art models such as the Spherical Fourier Neural Operator (FourCastNet v2), GraphCast, and Aurora, and design experiments that integrate data assimilation frameworks into the inference stage of these models to improve the alignment of forecasts with physical observations. Building on these experiments, I created an Observing System Simulation Experiments (OSSEs) framework for AI-based weather models to assess how new satellite observing systems and assimilation strategies can influence the performance of AI-based forecasting systems, and to evaluate how assimilating satellite data with AI models can improve long-range prediction and demonstrate their potential in operational forecasting.

As part of my Master’s thesis, I am training FourCastNet v2 from scratch on reanalysis datasets to study how physics-informed neural operators learn atmospheric dynamics, evaluate their ability to generalize across variables and timescales, and improve the skill of long-range prediction. Beyond analyzing learning dynamics, my work aims to advance AI-based Earth system forecasting by training FourCastNet v2 from scratch to build a controllable and extensible model that serves as a scalable foundation for integrating data assimilation and designing OSSE-based experiments. This research contributes toward the development of foundation models that integrate numerical weather prediction (NWP) and AI, combining physical principles with data-driven learning.

More broadly, my interests lie in the application of artificial intelligence, machine learning, and deep learning across scientific and engineering domains. With a strong foundation in neural network architectures—including physics-informed models, neural operators, and transformers—I am particularly drawn to the emerging field of Scientific Machine Learning (SciML). In this context, I have gained experience working with large-scale AI models for climate forecasting, which has allowed me to study their training dynamics and scalability.
